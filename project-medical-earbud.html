Engineering at the Frontier

At Worcester Polytechnic Institute, I'm leading the design and development of a wearable medical earbud capable of tracking vital signs with clinical-grade accuracy — all from a discreet, comfortable device worn behind the ear.

The device targets the sensitive region beneath the ear near the jaw — a site identified in recent biomedical research as an exceptionally rich source of physiological data. From this single contact point, the earbud monitors blood pressure, heart rate, respiratory patterns, and vocal activity simultaneously.

The mechanical design, developed in AutoCAD, prioritizes ergonomics and comfort: the housing must be lightweight, anatomically contoured, and robust enough for continuous wear. The embedded firmware, written in C++, processes sensor data in real time and manages power efficiency for extended battery life.

Key Contributions

  • Spearheaded the design and development of a wearable medical earbud capable of tracking vital signs including blood pressure, heart rate, respiratory patterns, and vocal activity.
  • Identified and targeted the sub-auricular jaw region as the sensing site based on recent physiological research showing its superior data density compared to conventional locations.
  • Collaborated with a WPI professor to lead the project from initial concept through functional prototype — managing both the mechanical design (AutoCAD) and embedded programming (C++).
  • Designed the device housing for minimal weight, anatomical comfort, and durability under daily-wear conditions.
  • Pursuing a formal patent for the final design and co-authoring a research paper for submission to the MIT Undergraduate Research Technology Conference (URTC).

This project sits at the intersection of mechanical engineering, biomedical sensing, and embedded systems — disciplines I'm merging for the first time at this scale. The goal is a device that doesn't just measure vitals but does so with enough accuracy and comfort to be used continuously in real-world conditions.
